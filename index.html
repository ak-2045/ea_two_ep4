Chapter 3
The sun rose earlier than anyone had expected—pale at first, then spilling into a strange, diluted brightness that made the morning feel both awake and half-asleep. It was not the sharp gold of a normal day, but a softened glow, as though the world itself had not fully decided to return. Akmal stirred beneath it, dragging himself out of bed with the heaviness of a night that had offered little rest. His eyes burned faintly, his movements slow, like someone still tethered to uneasy dreams. Harshita, in contrast, stood already awake—alert, composed, and quietly resolute. She could do this.Not because she had slept well—far from it. She hadn’t slept a wink. And yet, the certainty remained. The thought had settled firmly in her chest overnight. Whatever this place was, however impossible it seemed, she would find a way out. And when she did, she promised herself something almost stubbornly normal: she would go back, meet her boyfriend, and spend the rest of the day pretending none of this had ever happened. Because none of it made sense. One moment, she had simply clicked a link Akmal had sent. And then—everything. Fragments returned unwillingly. Akmal’s voice, sharp with panic: *“Fuck—I messed up. I connected my dream world to the neural link…”* Then the sensation of being pulled—no, dragged—into something that didn’t feel like space or time at all. She pushed the memory away. It left behind a faint wave of nausea, the kind that lingered unpleasantly at the back of her throat. Even the food here had betrayed her. Hogwarts—or whatever version of it this was—had clearly not been designed with vegetarians in mind. She had gone to bed hungry, irritated, and determined. Now, she dressed quickly in her robes, fingers moving with purpose. The night had not been wasted. She had spent hours thinking—planning. Harry would need help with the tasks. Maybe more than help. Maybe intervention. And beyond all of that, there was the real objective—the only one that truly mattered. The password. Somewhere in this world, hidden beneath its spells and stories, was the key to leaving it. And she intended to find it. — Outside, the air carried a cool stillness as the participants gathered near a large tent. The fabric rippled gently, whispering in the faint morning breeze. Nearby, Hermione Granger and Neville Longbottom stood together, deep in discussion—voices low, urgent, as though rehearsing strategies one final time. Akmal joined Harshita, rubbing the back of his neck, his expression already defeated. “This body won’t allow me to do wonders,” he muttered. “You’re going to have to carry us. I don’t even remember any spells from the books.” He paused, exhaling. “Plus… I’m not really the wand type. More like… punch first, think later.” Harshita glanced at him, and for a moment—just a moment—her lips curved into something almost amused.
